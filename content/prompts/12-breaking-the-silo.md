## Purpose

Expand the audience beyond legal ops. This article speaks to CFOs, business unit leaders, and executives — anyone affected by legal data silos. It positions LOI as a cross-functional capability, not just a legal department tool.

## Card Summary / Why This Matters

> Legal does not operate in isolation — yet most legal technology does. Contracts affect revenue recognition. Litigation impacts financial forecasts. Regulatory matters shape business strategy. When legal operates as a silo, the entire organization makes decisions with incomplete information. Legal Operations Intelligence bridges this gap — not by adding another integration, but by creating a shared operational layer.

## Structure

### 1. Why This Matters (~150 words)
The value of legal operations extends far beyond the legal department. But realizing that value requires breaking down information barriers. Reference LOI (Article 1) as the framework that enables this cross-functional visibility.

### 2. The Silo Problem (~300 words)
How legal data is trapped:

- **Separate systems**: Legal uses one set of tools, finance uses another, business units use a third. No shared data layer.
- **Different taxonomies**: Legal categorizes work by matter type; finance by cost center; business by strategic priority. The same activity gets described three different ways.
- **No shared visibility**: Finance cannot see legal exposure in real time. Business units cannot access contract intelligence. Legal cannot connect its work to business outcomes.
- **One-way communication**: Legal reports upward (quarterly accruals, annual summaries) but business context rarely flows downward to inform legal priorities.

Concrete examples:
- The M&A team is evaluating a target but cannot easily see the target's litigation exposure without asking legal for a manual report
- Finance is forecasting Q3 expenses but does not have real-time visibility into the legal matter pipeline
- A business unit is negotiating a major contract but does not know that legal has negotiated similar terms with this counterparty before

### 3. What Cross-Functional Visibility Enables (~300 words)
Each example connects legal data to business outcomes:

- **Revenue teams with contract insight**: Contract renewal dates, obligation tracking, and term analysis visible to the business units that own the relationships
- **Finance with legal spend forecasts**: Real-time accrual estimates, predictive spend by matter type, budget vs. actual at the portfolio level — reference spend visibility from Article 10
- **Business units with regulatory context**: Regulatory matters and compliance obligations surfaced to the teams whose operations they affect
- **Executives with unified risk views**: Litigation exposure, contract risk, regulatory status — aggregated into a single executive dashboard
- **Procurement with outside counsel performance**: Law firm spend, matter outcomes, rate benchmarking — informing vendor decisions

### 4. The Platform Approach (~250 words)
Point integrations create brittle connections. Every API, sync job, or middleware layer is a fragile bridge between silos.

A platform approach is different:
- Built on infrastructure the whole organization already uses (M365) — reference Article 4
- Deployed in the organization's own tenant (Article 6) — shared data layer, not shared via integration
- Natural cross-functional visibility because legal, finance, and business already operate in the same M365 environment
- SharePoint, Teams, and Outlook are the communication layer; Spaarke adds the intelligence layer

The key insight: when your legal ops platform runs on the same infrastructure as the rest of the business, cross-functional visibility is architectural, not aspirational.

### 5. Starting the Conversation (~150 words)
How legal ops leaders can initiate cross-functional alignment:

- Identify one high-value data point that another department needs from legal (spend forecast, contract status, litigation exposure)
- Demonstrate what visibility looks like with that single data point
- Expand from there

Position as a leadership opportunity: the legal ops leader who breaks down these silos becomes a strategic partner to finance, business units, and the C-suite. This is not a technology project — it is a positioning move.
